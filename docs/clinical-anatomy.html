0The 9 services — color legend

The eye decomposed by which job each part does, not by anatomical chapter. Every disease drops into one (rarely two) of these. The same color is reused in every diagram below and in the markdown docs, so you build visual recognition. Click a card to expand.

Two planes carry the signal (data plane: optical → sensor → transport, plus coordination across both eyes); five support services keep that signal alive. Shared hardware — the uvea (iris + ciliary body + choroid) — means services aren't fully independent, which is why compound diseases like neovascular glaucoma exist.

1Eye — horizontal cross-section

The canonical cutaway. Light enters from the left (cornea) and lands on the retina at the back. Three concentric walls: fibrous (cornea + sclera), vascular (the uvea = iris + ciliary body + choroid), neural (retina). Tinted by service.

Optical path = cornea → aqueous → pupil → lens → vitreous → retina. The uvea (vascular choroid + ciliary body + iris) is one continuous tissue threading pressure, optical and vascular services — the reason inflammation in one zone spills into others.

2Anterior segment & aqueous flow — the glaucoma circuit

Same anatomy serves two readings: the optical front-end, and the pressure-service drainage loop. Aqueous is made by the ciliary body, flows forward through the pupil, and drains at the angle. Block the drain → intraocular pressure (IOP) climbs → optic nerve damage.

One circuit, two failure modes: open-angle (the meshwork sieve clogs — slow, silent) and angle-closure (the iris physically blocks the angle — can be acute and painful). Both raise IOP and damage the optic nerve (transport service).

3Retinal layers — light comes from the top, detection at the bottom

Counter-intuitive: light passes through the transparent neural layers first and is captured at the back by photoreceptors sitting on the RPE. OCT images these layers in cross-section. Three are load-bearing: RNFL, photoreceptors, RPE.

★ = the three load-bearing layers. Mnemonic for disease mapping: RNFL thins in glaucoma (transport), photoreceptors fail in retinitis pigmentosa, RPE degenerates in AMD. The inner retina is fed by the central retinal artery; the outer retina (photoreceptors + RPE) by the choroid — two supplies, two failure modes (see §5).

4Macula topology — concentric zones & cone falloff

The macula is the high-resolution sensor at the optical axis. Cone density peaks at the foveola and drops steeply outward — which is why only the central few degrees give sharp reading vision, and why macular disease is so disabling even when the rest of the retina is fine.

Two clinical anchors: the foveal avascular zone (graded on OCT-A) and the steep cone falloff that makes central acuity a macula test, not a whole-retina test. [CHECK] exact ring diameters vary slightly by source — values are textbook-typical.

5Two blood supplies — central retinal vs choroidal

The retina is fed by two independent trees. The central retinal artery feeds the inner retina (ganglion + bipolar); the choroid feeds the outer retina (photoreceptors + RPE). Two supplies → two distinct ischemic syndromes.

Why it matters clinically: CRAO (inner-retina infarct) whitens the retina with a cherry-red spot; choroidal failure or neovascularization hits photoreceptors/RPE (AMD). The optic nerve head has its own supply (posterior ciliary) — its infarct is NAION (see neuro-ophth deep-dive).

6Visual pathway & field defects — lesion localization

The single most useful neuro-ophthalmology diagram. Each point along the pathway, when damaged, produces a characteristic visual-field defect. The chiasm is the hinge: nasal fibers cross there, so anything behind it affects both eyes' fields on the same side.

Rule of thumb: defects in one eye = lesion at or before the chiasm. Homonymous (both eyes, same side) = behind the chiasm. Congruity increases the further back you go; macular sparing points to the occipital cortex.

7Extraocular muscles & the H-pattern

Six muscles per eye, three cranial nerves. The doctor traces an "H" (or double-H) to test each muscle in its field of action. Diplopia + the direction it worsens localizes the weak muscle and its nerve.

Color = innervating nerve: CN III (four muscles + lid + pupil), CN IV (superior oblique only), CN VI (lateral rectus only). Mnemonic LR6SO4 — all the rest are CN III.

8Cranial nerves for the eye — who does what

Five cranial nerves plus the sympathetic chain run the eye. This is the quick map of what each carries and how its lesion shows up at the slit lamp.

Pupil cheat-sheet: big pupil that won't constrict + ptosis + down-and-out = CN III; small pupil + ptosis = Horner (sympathetic); afferent defect (RAPD) = CN II / retina. Blink reflex tests CN V in, CN VII out.
